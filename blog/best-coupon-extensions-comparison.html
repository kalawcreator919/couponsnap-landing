Honey: The Giant With a Tarnished Reputation

Honey was acquired by PayPal for $4 billion in 2020, and it's the most-installed coupon extension on Chrome. But in late 2024, investigative reports from major YouTubers exposed serious problems.

The Honey controversy, explained:

  • Affiliate hijacking: Honey allegedly replaced content creators' affiliate tracking codes at checkout — meaning creators lost commissions on purchases they drove.
  • Suboptimal codes: Honey was accused of surfacing lower-value codes to maximize its own affiliate revenue, not the user's savings.
  • PayPal data integration: Your shopping behavior feeds directly into PayPal's data ecosystem.

Honey still works, and it covers the most stores. But you're paying for it with your data — and potentially your trust in creators you support.

Capital One Shopping: The Bank's Coupon Play

Capital One Shopping (formerly Wikibuy) is a capable extension that auto-applies coupons and compares prices across retailers. You don't need a Capital One account to use it.

Pros:

  • Good coupon database (15,000+ stores)
  • Price comparison feature is genuinely useful
  • No credit card required

Cons:

  • Backed by a major bank — data use is extensive
  • Account creation encouraged
  • Rewards program requires Capital One card for full value
